# Analyze Game Positioning with SWOT and VRIO

## Responsibility

Support a strategic choice with evidence-linked SWOT and VRIO analysis. Use this skill to decide where to position, invest, simplify, protect, test, or stop. Do not present framework labels as conclusions.

Do not use it to invent demand, forecast revenue, or copy a competitor's feature list. Route the concept and PoC itself to `developing-game-concepts-and-pocs`; route in-game repeat behavior to `designing-game-core-loops`.

## Evidence discipline

State the decision before collecting evidence. Separate:

- verified current facts;
- internal capabilities and constraints;
- hypotheses;
- player evidence;
- recommendations.

Research time-sensitive market, platform, pricing, competitor, and launch claims from current primary sources when they affect the result. Treat player reviews as signals to test, not proof of the project’s own demand.

## 1. Establish the comparison frame

| Field | Define |
|---|---|
| Decision | Choice to make now |
| Target segment | Player need, behavior, platform, and time or payment commitment |
| Player job | Experience or problem the player hires the game to provide |
| Alternatives | 3–5 games, adjacent entertainment, or non-play substitutes |
| Value proposition | Reason to try, continue, and recommend |
| Scope and horizon | Team, budget, region, launch window, and post-launch assumptions |
| Proof required | Evidence that can confirm or invalidate the direction |

Select alternatives by the same player job, not merely the same genre.

## 3. Test the real assets with VRIO

Evaluate a concrete resource, capability, pipeline, relationship, technology, or operating practice. Do not evaluate a vague claim such as “fun gameplay.”

| Asset | Value | Rarity | Imitability | Organization | Result | Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
|  | Player or business value | Competitors lacking it at needed quality or timing | Cost, time, or difficulty to reproduce | Ownership, process, budget, tools, and access | parity / temporary / potential / sustained / disadvantage |  |

Use these interpretations:

- **Parity:** a table stake; do not center positioning on it.
- **Temporary advantage:** exploit timing and add protection or operational capacity.
- **Potential advantage:** establish ownership and execution before promising it publicly.
- **Sustained advantage:** center the position and protect the producing system.
- **Disadvantage:** cut, reframe, or stop investing.

An original mechanic is not automatically hard to imitate. Test whether players receive a non-substitutable benefit and whether the team can deliver it repeatedly.

## 4. Decide and test the position

Write a falsifiable statement:

> For **[target segment]** who want **[player job]**, **[game]** is a **[understandable category]** that delivers **[distinct benefit]** through **[credible differentiator]**. Unlike **[alternatives]**, it **[evidence-backed difference]**.

Then make each claim operational.

| Position claim | Production capability that must deliver it | First validation | Success threshold | Risk / dependency | Do not build yet |

## Adversarial review

Challenge the decision:

- Would target players actually name the same alternatives and value the stated difference?
- Is an “opportunity” merely a broad audience claim without unmet-need evidence?
- Was an external trend misclassified as a team strength?
- Can a funded competitor copy the benefit in one update?
- Does the advantage depend on an unsecured person, partner, license, data source, or platform?
- Can the team operate the differentiator after launch, not merely demonstrate it once?
- Which failed assumption would most change the recommendation? Make it the first test.

## Output contract

Return:

1. decision and comparison frame;
2. evidence-tagged SWOT;
3. up to three strategic moves;
4. VRIO analysis of assets that support those moves;
5. positioning statement;
6. validation plan, thresholds, risks, and deferred work.
